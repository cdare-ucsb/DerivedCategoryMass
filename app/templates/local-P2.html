Local $\mathbb{P}^2$

The extra dimensions that arise in string theory would need to be compact of a very tiny scale in order to avoid experimental detection; by additionally assuming that the theory admits a supersymmetry, we are in fact imposing a strong geometric condition on the compactified target space.

In order to have an unbroken $\mathcal{N} = 1$ supersymmetry in 4 spacetime dimensions, there must exist a spinor which is covariantly constant (i.e. unchanged under parallel transport). On complex threefolds, this is equivalent to the claim that the holonomy of the underlying target space is $SU(3)$ — in other words, the manifold is Calabi-Yau.

A gauge field configuration is defined by a connection $A$ on a holomorphic vector bundle $E \to M$ over some complex manifold $M$. In this setting, vacuum solutions correspond to gauge field configurations which minimize the Yang-Mills action $$ S_{\operatorname{YM}} = \int_M \operatorname{Tr}(F_A \wedge \ast F_A) $$ In supersymmetric Yang-Mills theory, one can show that a minimum-energy configuration is achieved only when the connection $A$ is a Hermite-Einstein connection. By a theorem of Donaldson-Ulhenbeck-Yau, this is mathematically equivalent to the bundle $E$ being $\mu_H$-stable with respect to some Kähler class $H$.







In string theory, objects depending on some choice of Kähler class are often modified by world-sheet instanton corrections. In this context, the vacuaa are described as BPS vacua and correspond to Bridgeland-stable objects in the derived category of our compactified dimensions (i.e. the Calabi-Yau threefold). Unfortunately, only one example of a complex compact Kahler threefold that admits geometric Bridgeland stability conditions is known (c.f. Li (2019)). Instead, physicists often consider a simpler "localized" model by assuming that the compactified dimensions are fibred over a complex projective plane (pictured below). This model has been dubbed "Local $\mathbb{P}^2$" and the space of Bridgeland stability conditions on it was originally studied in Bayer, Macrì (2011).

$\ ^\star\ $ It should be noted that a "very general" compact complex Calabi-Yau does not contain a projective plane (i.e. by randomly choosing a CY 3-fold, mathematically there is a 0% chance you will pick one with an embedded $\mathbb{P}^2$).

The behavior of stability conditions on Local $\mathbb{P}^2$ is very closely related to those on the underlying projective plane (arising as the zero section of $\omega_{\mathbb{P}^2} \to \mathbb{P}^2$). Bridgeland stability conditions on $\mathbb{P}^2$ were classified by Li, (2017) and depend on the classical theory of slope-stable bundles on $\mathbb{P}^2$, as originally described by Drézet, Le Potier (1985).

In their seminal article, Drézet and Le Potier showed that a vector bundle $E \to \mathbb{P}^2$ is slope-stable if and only if the coordinates of its slope and discriminant $(x,y) = (\mu, \Delta)$ lie above a certain fractal curve. Following notation of Li, it is in some ways easier to consider the Drézet-Le Potier curve embedded in the $( \frac{\operatorname{ch}_1}{\operatorname{ch}_0}, \frac{\operatorname{ch}_2}{ \operatorname{ch}_0} )$-plane, as shown below.

It turns out that the set of "geometric" stability conditions on Local $\mathbb{P}^2$ looks precisely like the previous graph; up to a certain $\mathbb{C}$-action, all central charges $$Z_{\omega, B} = \int_{\mathbb{P}^2} e^{-B -i \omega}\operatorname{ch}\ \circ \ \pi_\ast $$ accounting for $B$-field and Kähler corrections can be reparameterized as $$ Z_{s, q} = (-\operatorname{ch}_2 + q \operatorname{ch}_0) + i (\operatorname{ch}_1 - s \operatorname{ch}_0) $$ where $(s,q)$ lies above the previous Drézet-Le Potier curve.



Unlike the standard projective plane, crossing any of the previous gray-walls leads to another identical chamber corresponding to a transition of BPS vacuum states. In this region, many of the previous stable BPS states will decompose and some of the previous unstable BPS states now become stable.




Each new "chamber" inherits its own set of walls, identical to the plot on the previous page. However, it is not true that crossing wall A (corresponding to the pushforward of exceptional $\mathcal{A} \in \operatorname{Coh}(\mathbb{P}^2)$) followed by crossing wall B (corresponding to exceptional $\mathcal{B} \in \operatorname{Coh}(\mathbb{P}^2)$) is the same as crossing wall B followed by A. One gets a "tree-like" structure of chambers with nodes corresponding to chambers and infinitely many branches at each point; however, there is a subtle identification of chambers that makes the geometry of the space of stability conditions on Local $\mathbb{P}^2$ signficantly more convoluted.

It was shown in my Ph.D. thesis that there is in fact an identification between many of the chambers above, leading to a tiling decomposition that is still not quite understood geometrically. However, it turns out that finding the mass of a standard line bundle in the other chambers is the same as finding the mass of a spherical twist in the original geometric chamber.
